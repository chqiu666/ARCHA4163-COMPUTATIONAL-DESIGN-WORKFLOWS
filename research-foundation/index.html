Agency

Telephonic Arm Wrestling (1986)

Norman White's pioneering telepresence installation explored distributed agency through physical competition across telecommunications networks. Participants in different locations could engage in arm wrestling matches, questioning the boundaries between local and remote agency.

The work challenged traditional notions of presence, control, and physical interaction, establishing early frameworks for understanding how agency can be distributed and mediated through technology.

Prediction

SEEK (1969-70)

Nicholas Negroponte's cybernetic installation at MIT placed gerbils and robotic intelligence in the same environment. The system attempted to predict and respond to unpredictable organic behavior, revealing the limits of computational prediction when confronted with living systems.

SEEK demonstrated the tension between programmed logic and biological agency, laying groundwork for understanding predictive interfaces and their inherent limitations.

Interaction

Mimus (2017)

Madeline Gannon's industrial robot installation transforms a 1200kg manufacturing machine into a curious, responsive creature. Mimus uses spatial awareness and body language to communicate with museum visitors, fostering empathy between humans and machines.

The project demonstrates how interactive design can create meaningful relationships between humans and autonomous systems, suggesting new possibilities for collaborative futures.

Methods & Approach

Computational Methods

Interactive installations, behavioral simulations, real-time sensor systems, and machine learning algorithms for understanding human-machine interaction patterns.

Quantitative Integration

Data visualization and behavioral metrics will be incorporated as part of the aesthetic and analytical framework, making the invisible dynamics of interaction visible.

Theoretical Framework

Drawing from cybernetics, human-computer interaction, media archaeology, and critical technology studies to understand socio-technical relationships.

Material Components

Physical installations, computational systems, aesthetic interfaces, and embodied interactions form the core material practice.

Future Trajectories

Toward Empathetic Human-Machine Collaboration

This research foundation explores the tension between human agency and machine prediction, seeking to understand how we might design more empathetic and collaborative relationships with autonomous systems.

Through computational design experiments and critical making practices, we investigate alternative futures where technology augments rather than replaces human creativity and intuition.

The challenge lies not in solving these tensions, but in creating spaces where they can be productively explored and negotiated.

Human-Centered Interaction

Collaborative Workspace Design

Understanding human behavior and spatial relationships in collaborative environments. This overhead perspective reveals the natural patterns of movement and interaction that occur when humans work alongside autonomous systems.

The design of interactive spaces must consider both human ergonomics and machine accessibility, creating environments where both agents can operate effectively while maintaining clear communication channels.

Technical Infrastructure

Mechanical Interface

The mechanical and technical components that enable human-machine collaboration require careful consideration of both functional requirements and interaction design. Each element must serve both operational and communicative purposes.

This exploded view demonstrates the complexity of creating devices that can bridge the gap between digital computation and physical interaction, requiring precision engineering and intuitive interface design.

Creative Collaboration

Expressive Automation

Exploring the boundaries between human creativity and machine precision through collaborative drawing processes. This investigation reveals how creative intent can be translated, interpreted, and expressed through mechanical systems.

The act of drawing becomes a dialogue between human intention and mechanical capability, creating new forms of expression that neither human nor machine could achieve independently.

Perspectives on Collaboration

Spatial Relationships

This lateral perspective offers insights into the spatial dynamics and ergonomic considerations essential for effective human-machine collaboration. The side view reveals the vertical relationships and accessibility patterns that shape interaction design.

Understanding these dimensional relationships is crucial for creating environments where humans and machines can work together naturally, maintaining both safety and efficiency while fostering meaningful collaborative experiences.
